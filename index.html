Universality

  • Some apparently unrelated critical systems can be described by the same CFT. This phenomenon is called critical universality. For instance, the same scaling dimension $\beta\simeq 0.33$ can be observed near the liquid/vapor and ferromagnetic/paramagnetic points of the second order phase transition: \[\begin{aligned} \rho_{\textrm{liquid}} - \rho_{\textrm{gas}} & = (T_c-T)^\beta\\ |\textbf{M}| & = (T_c-T)^\beta \end{aligned} \]

  • Some apparently unrelated critical systems can be described by the same CFT. This phenomenon is called critical universality. For instance, the same scaling dimension $\beta\simeq 0.33$ can be observed near the liquid/vapor and ferromagnetic/paramagnetic points of the second order phase transition: \[\begin{aligned} \rho_{\textrm{liquid}} - \rho_{\textrm{gas}} & = (T_c-T)^\beta\\ |\textbf{M}| & = (T_c-T)^\beta \end{aligned} \]
  • Two different microscopic theories can end up at the same fixed point of an RG flow, if the difference between them boils down to irrelevant operators. Therefore at the fixed point they will be described by the same CFT, manifesting critical universality [Wilson, Fisher, 1972]. Such models are said to be in the same universality class.

To avoid confusion, from now on we will denote short-range degrees of freedom with capital letters.
